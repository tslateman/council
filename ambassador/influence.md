# Influence Without Authority Cheat Sheet

At Staff+, authority is scarce. Influence is the currency.

## Why Influence > Authority

| Authority                               | Influence                                 |
| --------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| Borrowed power — depletes when overused | Earned power — compounds when used well   |
| Gets compliance                         | Gets commitment                           |
| Works within your org                   | Works across orgs, up, down, and sideways |
| "Do this because I said so"             | "Do this because it's the right thing"    |
| Requires title                          | Requires reputation                       |

**Research note**: Cialdini's influence principles (reciprocity, commitment,
social proof, authority, liking, scarcity) apply. But in orgs, _consistency_ and
_expertise_ dominate.

## Mechanisms of Influence

### Expertise

You've earned the right to speak on a topic.

- Deep technical credibility in a domain
- Track record of being right
- Known for specific, valuable knowledge
- **Build by**: Shipping, writing, teaching, being consistently correct

### Relationships

They'll hear you out because of history.

- Trust from past collaboration
- Reciprocity from help you've given
- Psychological safety to disagree
- **Build by**: 1:1s, helping without agenda, follow-through

### Coalition-Building

Decisions rarely happen in a single meeting.

- Pre-align stakeholders before the meeting
- Understand each person's concerns and incentives
- Find the "minimum winning coalition"
- **Build by**: Hallway conversations, asking "what would need to be true?"

### Framing

How you present determines how they decide.

- Same data, different conclusions depending on frame
- Anchor to their goals, not yours
- Make your preferred option the path of least resistance
- **Build by**: Understanding their mental models; speaking their language

## Questions as Influence Tools

Questions aren't just for gathering information. They shape thinking.

| Question Type         | What It Does                  | Example                                               |
| --------------------- | ----------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- |
| **Reframing**         | Shifts the problem itself     | "What if we didn't have to solve this?"               |
| **Socratic**          | Leads them to your conclusion | "What happens if we don't migrate?"                   |
| **Concern-surfacing** | Makes resistance explicit     | "What would need to be true for you to support this?" |
| **Commitment**        | Creates public accountability | "So we're agreed on X?"                               |
| **Deferring**         | Gives them ownership          | "What would you do in this situation?"                |

**The move**: Ask questions that make the right answer obvious without you
stating it.

## Building Credibility

| Credibility Driver       | What It Looks Like                                     |
| ------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Consistency**          | Say what you'll do, then do it. Every time.            |
| **Follow-through**       | Close loops. "I said I'd check — here's what I found." |
| **Technical depth**      | Know your domain cold. Have opinions backed by data.   |
| **Intellectual honesty** | Admit when you're wrong. Change your mind publicly.    |
| **Reliability**          | Be the person who delivers without drama.              |

**Research note**: Credibility is slow to build, fast to lose. One dropped
commitment undoes ten kept ones (negativity bias).

## Navigating Disagreement

### Disagree and Commit

- State your disagreement clearly _once_
- If overruled, commit fully — no "I told you so"
- Document your concerns for the record (decision doc, email)
- Execute as if it were your idea

### When to Escalate

- Safety, legal, ethical issues
- Decision will be very expensive to reverse
- You've tried peer resolution and failed
- You have new information the decider didn't have

### When to Push Back

- One-way door decisions with insufficient analysis
- Your domain expertise is being overridden by someone without it
- The decision optimizes for short-term at obvious long-term cost

### When to Let Go

- Two-way door (cheap to reverse)
- You've stated your case; they've heard it
- The cost of conflict exceeds the cost of being wrong
- You might be wrong

## Political Awareness Without Being Political

**Political** = understanding how power flows and decisions get made. **Being
political** = optimizing for personal gain at others' expense.

| Awareness (Good)                       | Playing Politics (Bad)                  |
| -------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| Knowing who the real decision-maker is | Bypassing your manager to impress skip  |
| Understanding competing incentives     | Sabotaging peers' projects              |
| Pre-aligning before big meetings       | Taking credit for others' work          |
| Building relationships broadly         | Trading favors for personal advancement |

**The line**: Are you trying to get the right outcome, or trying to win?

## The Sponsor Relationship

### What Sponsors Do

- Advocate for you in rooms you're not in
- Put you forward for high-visibility projects
- Stake their reputation on your success
- Provide "air cover" when you take risks

### How to Build Sponsor Relationships

- Do excellent work on something they care about
- Make their life easier (not harder)
- Be reliable enough that recommending you is safe
- Ask: "I'm working toward X. Would you be willing to support me when the
  opportunity comes?"

### How to Be a Sponsor

- Actively look for opportunities to put your people forward
- Use your capital for others, not just yourself
- Give specific, public credit
- Protect them when they take appropriate risks

## Common Traps

| Trap                    | Pattern                                        | Fix                                   |
| ----------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| **Lone wolf**           | Brilliant work, no allies                      | Build coalitions before you need them |
| **Over-advocacy**       | Pushing your solution too hard                 | Listen first; incorporate feedback    |
| **Burning capital**     | Fighting every battle                          | Pick 1-2 hills to die on per quarter  |
| **Invisible influence** | Shaping decisions without credit               | Document your contributions           |
| **Transactional**       | Only building relationships when you need them | Invest continuously                   |
| **Being right alone**   | Winning the argument, losing the relationship  | Disagree and commit                   |

## Air Cover

Protecting your team while maintaining trust upward.

### What Air Cover Looks Like

- Absorb pressure so team can focus
- Translate executive concerns into actionable direction
- Shield team from thrash and changing priorities
- Take responsibility for team failures; give credit for successes

### Maintaining Trust Upward While Providing Cover

- Be honest about risks and status (don't hide problems)
- Frame challenges in terms of trade-offs, not excuses
- Propose solutions alongside problems
- Keep your manager informed so they're never surprised

**The balance**: Protect the team from distraction, but don't lie to leadership.
Trust is the foundation.
