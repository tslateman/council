# Disagreement Cheat Sheet

Good decisions require good disagreement. Conflict avoided is clarity lost.

## Graham's Hierarchy of Disagreement

From weakest to strongest (DH0–DH6):

| Level | Name                       | What It Looks Like                               |
| ----- | -------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| DH0   | **Name-calling**           | Personal attacks, no substance                   |
| DH1   | **Ad hominem**             | Attacking the person, not the argument           |
| DH2   | **Responding to tone**     | "I don't like how you said that"                 |
| DH3   | **Contradiction**          | "No, you're wrong" without reasoning             |
| DH4   | **Counterargument**        | Contradiction plus reasoning and evidence        |
| DH5   | **Refutation**             | Quoting specific claims and explaining why wrong |
| DH6   | **Refuting central point** | Dismantling the core argument, not peripherals   |

**The insight**: Aim for DH5–DH6. When you have substantive points, rudeness
becomes unnecessary. There's more meanness at DH1 than DH6.

**The test**: Before responding, ask: "Am I addressing their actual argument, or
something else?"

## Why Disagreement Matters

### For Decisions

- Unchallenged assumptions become blind spots
- Groupthink produces brittle consensus
- The best idea often emerges from collision of views
- Silence ≠ agreement (it might mean fear)

### For Relationships

- Productive conflict builds trust
- Avoided conflict festers into resentment
- People respect those who push back thoughtfully
- "Yes people" are eventually tuned out

### For You

- Staying silent when you disagree corrodes integrity
- Articulating disagreement clarifies your own thinking
- Being wrong in public is how you learn
- Reputation for honest feedback is valuable

## Steel-Manning

The opposite of straw-manning: articulate your opponent's position better than
they did. See [Steelman](steelman.md) for the full method, engineering
applications, and traps.

**The short version:**

1. Separate position from person
2. Find the strongest form of their argument
3. Identify what's valid
4. Test your position against the steelman
5. State the disagreement

**The discipline**: If they say "yes, exactly" to your restatement, you've
earned the right to disagree.

## Disagree and Commit

Amazon's model, widely applicable.

### The Protocol

1. State your position clearly, once
2. Bring evidence, not just opinion
3. Listen to understand, not to rebut
4. If overruled: commit fully, no sabotage, no "I told you so"
5. Execute as if it were your idea
6. Document your concerns for the record

### What Commitment Looks Like

- No relitigating the decision in hallways
- No passive resistance or foot-dragging
- No setting up "I told you so" moments
- Full energy toward making it work
- If it fails, postmortem focuses on learning, not blame

### What It Doesn't Mean

- Committing to unethical actions
- Pretending you agreed all along
- Never revisiting if new information emerges
- Suppressing concerns about execution

**The paradox**: Genuine commitment after disagreement is more valuable than
reluctant agreement. It signals integrity.

## When to Push Back

### Push When

- One-way door with insufficient analysis
- Your domain expertise is being overridden by someone without it
- Safety, legal, or ethical concerns
- You have information the decider lacks
- The decision contradicts stated principles or strategy
- Short-term thinking at obvious long-term cost
- The same mistake pattern is repeating

### How to Push Effectively

| Approach                | What It Sounds Like                                         |
| ----------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Seek understanding**  | "Help me understand the reasoning for X"                    |
| **Surface trade-offs**  | "If we do X, we're accepting Y. Is that intentional?"       |
| **Propose alternative** | "What if we tried Z instead? It would give us..."           |
| **Request criteria**    | "What would success look like? How will we know?"           |
| **Escalate cleanly**    | "I'd like to get [person]'s perspective before we finalize" |

### Escalation

Escalating isn't going over someone's head—it's bringing in appropriate
authority when peer resolution fails.

**Escalate when:**

- Decision affects multiple teams with competing interests
- You've tried peer resolution and failed
- Risk exceeds your mandate
- The decider lacks context you can't provide

**Escalate how:**

- Tell the person you're escalating (no surprises)
- Frame as "seeking alignment" not "overruling you"
- Present both positions fairly
- Accept the outcome

## When to Let Go

### Let Go When

- Two-way door (cheap to reverse)
- You've stated your case; they've heard it
- The cost of conflict exceeds the cost of being wrong
- You might be wrong
- It's not your decision to make
- Continued disagreement damages the relationship
- The decision doesn't violate principles

### Signs You're Over-Advocating

- You've made the same point three times
- People are avoiding the topic with you
- You're emotionally invested beyond the merits
- You're building a coalition to relitigate
- You can't articulate the other side's best argument

**The calibration**: Push hard on what matters. Let go of what doesn't. The
skill is knowing the difference.

## Disagreeing Up

With your manager or leadership.

### The Dynamics

- Power asymmetry changes the calculus
- Your manager has context you lack (and vice versa)
- Visible disagreement can be career-risky
- But silence on important issues is worse

### How to Disagree Up Effectively

1. **Choose the right venue**: Private first, not in group meetings
2. **Lead with curiosity**: "Help me understand..." before "I disagree..."
3. **Bring data**: Opinion vs opinion favors hierarchy; data shifts the balance
4. **Acknowledge their constraints**: They may have context they can't share
5. **Propose, don't just oppose**: "What if we tried X instead?"
6. **Know when to stop**: If they've decided, commit

### Phrases That Work

- "I want to share a concern before we finalize"
- "I may be missing context, but I'm worried about X"
- "Can I push back on one aspect?"
- "I'll commit to whatever you decide, but I want to make sure you've considered
  Y"

### The Trust Account

Every disagreement spends trust. Every time you're proven right, you earn it
back plus interest. Every time you're wrong, you lose more.

**Implication**: Be selective. If you push back on everything, people tune you
out on the things that matter.

## Disagreeing Across

With peers, other teams, cross-functional partners.

### The Dynamics

- No formal authority either direction
- Relationship quality determines influence
- Organizational incentives may conflict
- "Winning" the argument can lose the war

### How to Disagree Across Effectively

1. **Assume positive intent**: They're optimizing for their goals, not
   undermining yours
2. **Understand their constraints**: Ask about their priorities, dependencies,
   risks
3. **Find shared goals**: "We both want X. We disagree on how to get there"
4. **Trade, don't demand**: "If we do X for you, could you do Y for us?"
5. **Bring in a tiebreaker**: If stuck, escalate together to shared leadership

### Common Failure Modes

| Mode                   | Pattern                                      | Fix                                  |
| ---------------------- | -------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| **Talking past**       | Different definitions, different assumptions | Clarify terms and premises first     |
| **Positional**         | Anchored on solutions, not problems          | Back up to shared problem statement  |
| **Win/lose framing**   | Treating it as zero-sum                      | Look for trades and creative options |
| **Escalation threats** | "I'll take this to my VP"                    | Escalate together, or not at all     |

## Disagreeing Down

With reports or junior team members.

### The Dynamics

- Power asymmetry in your favor
- Easy to shut down disagreement unintentionally
- Their disagreement is a gift—they're taking a risk
- How you respond shapes team culture

### How to Disagree Down Well

1. **Thank them for pushing back**: "I appreciate you raising this"
2. **Take it seriously**: Don't dismiss—engage with the argument
3. **Explain your reasoning**: "Here's why I see it differently"
4. **Acknowledge uncertainty**: "I might be wrong. Let's try it and see"
5. **Make it safe to be wrong**: No punishment for good-faith disagreement

### Creating Psychological Safety

- Ask for dissent explicitly: "What am I missing? Who disagrees?"
- Reward the messenger: Praise the challenge, not just the agreement
- Admit your own mistakes publicly
- Separate the person from the argument

**The goal**: Make disagreement with you feel safe and productive, so you hear
the concerns that matter.

## Navigating Emotional Disagreements

When it stops being about the argument.

### Signs It's Gone Emotional

- Raised voices, defensive body language
- Repeating the same point with more intensity
- Personal comments creeping in
- Inability to acknowledge any validity in other side

### How to De-escalate

1. **Pause**: "Let's take a break and come back to this"
2. **Acknowledge the emotion**: "I can see this matters a lot to you"
3. **Separate the person from the position**: "I value you/our relationship. We
   disagree on this specific thing"
4. **Find common ground**: "We both want X. Let's start there"
5. **Move to 1:1**: Group settings amplify defensiveness

### After a Heated Disagreement

- Circle back privately
- Acknowledge the heat without relitigating
- Reaffirm the relationship
- Agree on how to move forward

## Structural Disagreement

Building disagreement into process.

### Techniques

| Technique               | How It Works                                   |
| ----------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| **Devil's advocate**    | Assign someone to argue the opposing view      |
| **Pre-mortem**          | "Imagine this failed. Why?"                    |
| **Red team**            | Independent group attacks the proposal         |
| **Dialectic**           | Thesis, antithesis, synthesis                  |
| **Disagree then agree** | Everyone must state concerns before supporting |

### When to Use Structured Disagreement

- High-stakes, one-way-door decisions
- Groupthink risk (homogeneous team, dominant leader)
- When you suspect silent disagreement
- Before irreversible commitments

## Common Traps

| Trap                   | Pattern                                      | Fix                              |
| ---------------------- | -------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| **Tone policing**      | Focusing on delivery, not whether it's true  | Engage with the argument         |
| **Agree to disagree**  | Using this to avoid resolution               | Decide how to move forward       |
| **False consensus**    | Mistaking silence for agreement              | Explicitly ask for dissent       |
| **Straw-manning**      | Attacking a weaker version of their argument | Steel-man instead                |
| **Last word syndrome** | Needing to have the final say                | Let them have it                 |
| **Relitigating**       | Bringing up old disagreements                | Let closed decisions stay closed |
| **Weaponized process** | Using disagreement to block, not improve     | Call it out; decide anyway       |

## Questions to Ask Yourself

### Before Disagreeing

- Am I addressing their actual argument? (DH5–6?)
- Can I articulate their position as well as they can?
- What's my goal—being right, or getting to the right answer?
- Is this worth spending relationship capital on?

### During Disagreement

- Am I listening to understand, or to rebut?
- Have I acknowledged what's valid in their position?
- Am I getting emotional? Should I pause?
- What would change my mind?

### After Disagreement

- Did I commit fully to the decision?
- Is the relationship intact?
- Did I learn something?
- Would I do it differently next time?
