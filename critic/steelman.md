# The Steelman Method

Build the strongest version of the argument you oppose. A shared method across
all seats -- the Critic's primary tool, available wherever a position needs
stress-testing before commitment.

## What Steelmanning Is / Isn't

| Steelmanning Is                                  | Steelmanning Isn't                          |
| ------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------- |
| Articulating their best case better than they do | Restating their words back to them          |
| Finding the strongest version of a weak argument | Pretending every argument is equally strong |
| Testing your position against the hardest case   | Softening your critique                     |
| Genuine effort to understand why they believe it | Performing fairness before dismissing       |
| A constraint that sharpens your thinking         | A courtesy that delays your rebuttal        |
| Discovering when they're right                   | Assuming they must be right                 |

**The test:** Could the person you disagree with read your steelman and say
"that's stronger than how I put it"? If not, you haven't steelmanned -- you've
paraphrased.

## Why It Works

Steelmanning is self-interested, not generous. It makes your own argument
better.

| Benefit                     | Mechanism                                                                    |
| --------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Sharpens your position**  | Defeating a strong argument proves more than defeating a weak one            |
| **Surfaces blind spots**    | Building their case forces you to see what you were ignoring                 |
| **Disarms defensiveness**   | People stop defending when they feel understood                              |
| **Reveals hidden validity** | Some arguments are right for reasons their author can't articulate           |
| **Builds credibility**      | Critics who steelman earn the right to be heard                              |
| **Prevents straw-manning**  | You can't accidentally attack a weaker version if you've built the strongest |

## The Method

### Step 1: Separate Position from Person

Strip away delivery, tone, credentials, and history. Extract the claim.

"They're saying X because they're junior" becomes "the claim is X."

### Step 2: Find the Strongest Form

Ask four questions:

1. **What context would make this the right answer?** Every wrong answer is
   right somewhere. Find where.
2. **What concrete evidence supports this?** Data, case studies, failure
   histories, constraint analyses -- not hypothetical appeal. If evidence exists
   and you didn't cite it, the steelman is incomplete.
3. **What would a domain expert who holds this view say?** Not the weakest
   advocate -- the strongest. Ground their case in specific evidence and
   constraints, not in what you imagine they'd feel.
4. **What positions exist beyond this binary?** A-vs-B framing hides options C,
   D, and E. Steelmanning one side can accidentally strawman a third.

### Step 3: Identify What's Valid

Separate the parts that hold from the parts that don't. Most wrong positions
contain correct observations. Name them.

### Step 4: Test Your Position Against the Steelman

Your rebuttal must address the strongest form, not the original. If your
argument only defeats the weak version, it's insufficient.

### Step 5: State the Disagreement

Now you've earned it. Structure as:

- "The strongest case for X is..."
- "That case holds because..."
- "Where it breaks down is..."
- "Therefore..."

## Engineering Applications

| Context                   | What to Steelman                                                     |
| ------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Architecture review**   | The proposal you'd reject -- what problem does it solve perfectly?   |
| **Tech debt debate**      | The case for not fixing it -- what's the opportunity cost of fixing? |
| **Tool/framework choice** | The option you dislike -- where does it outperform your preference?  |
| **Code review**           | The approach you'd change -- what constraint led to this design?     |
| **Incident postmortem**   | The decision that failed -- what made it reasonable at the time?     |
| **Migration proposal**    | The case for staying -- what's the real cost of switching?           |

## Steelmanning in Practice

### Solo Decision-Making

Before committing to a direction, write the steelman for the path you're
rejecting. If you can't construct a compelling case against your own choice, you
haven't explored the space.

**Template:**

```text
I'm choosing A over B.
The strongest case for B is: [steelman]
B is right that: [valid points]
A wins because: [specific advantage over the steelman, not the weak version]
```

### In Critique

Rapoport's Rules (see [Critique](critique.md)) encode steelmanning as steps 1-3.
The steelman is the restatement that earns the right to rebut.

### In Disagreement

Steel-manning transforms disagreement from combat to collaboration. See
[Disagreement](disagreement.md) for the full context on productive conflict.

## Common Traps

| Trap                          | Pattern                                                                 | Fix                                                          |
| ----------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Steelman as stalling**      | Using the process to delay decisions you should make now                | Time-box it. Two-way doors don't need steelmanning           |
| **Steelman as capitulation**  | Building their case so well you lose confidence in your own             | The steelman tests your position; it doesn't replace it      |
| **Performative steelmanning** | Going through the motions without genuine engagement                    | If you can't name what you learned, you faked it             |
| **Steelmanning everything**   | Applying the same rigor to trivial and critical decisions               | Reserve for one-way doors and high-stakes choices            |
| **Steelmanning bad faith**    | Building charitable interpretations of deliberately dishonest arguments | Steelman the argument, not the arguer's intent               |
| **Partial steelman**          | Restating only the parts you can easily defeat                          | Ask: "Would they endorse this as their best case?"           |
| **Straw-steelmanning**        | Replacing the position with a more palatable one you prefer             | The original advocate must recognize your steelman as theirs |

## Questions

### Before Steelmanning

- What's the actual claim, stripped of delivery and personality?
- Am I genuinely curious about why someone smart would hold this view?
- Is this a high-enough-stakes decision to warrant the effort?

### During Steelmanning

- Would the strongest advocate of this position endorse my steelman?
- What evidence am I ignoring because it supports their view?
- Have I found the context where this position is obviously correct?

### After Steelmanning

- Did I learn something I didn't know before?
- Does my rebuttal address the strongest form, or the original?
- Am I more or less confident in my position?
